The observed failure to achieve high-fidelity reconstruction at high multipoles ($\ell > 3000$), as evidenced by the low transfer function $T(\ell)$, is directly attributable to the constraints described in the dataset:

1. **Beam Convolution and Resolution Limits**: The dataset description specifies that the stacked signal maps are smoothed with frequency-specific beams (FWHM 2.2, 1.4, and 1.0 arcmin for SO LAT; 4.5–4.72 arcmin for Planck). The information loss at $\ell > 3000$ is a direct consequence of the beam-induced suppression of high-frequency signal components, which renders the 1-arcmin ground truth unrecoverable in the observed data.
2. **Instrumental Noise Dominance**: The dataset provides specific noise power spectra for the SO LAT and Planck FFP10 realizations. The analysis results correctly identify that at small angular scales, the instrumental noise power exponentially dominates the signal power. The "catastrophic noise amplification" noted in the results is a direct consequence of the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) dropping below unity at high multipoles, a constraint inherent to the provided noise models.

These constraints limit the analysis by imposing a fundamental information-theoretic ceiling on deterministic, pixel-perfect reconstruction, explaining why the SR-DAE must prioritize spectral consistency over phase-accurate recovery at small scales.
