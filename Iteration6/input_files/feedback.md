The current analysis successfully demonstrates that a non-linear SR-DAE outperforms linear cILC in MSE and bias, particularly by avoiding the noise amplification inherent in deconvolution. However, the project is currently stalled by a "deterministic trap": the model is being penalized for failing to predict high-frequency phases that are fundamentally unrecoverable due to the beam/noise floor.

**Critical Weaknesses & Gaps:**
1. **The "Transfer Function" Misinterpretation:** You are currently using $T(\ell)$ as a measure of "success." In a denoising task with high noise, $T(\ell) \to 0$ is expected. Using this to claim "failure" is misleading. You are effectively measuring the *predictability* of the signal, not the *quality* of the reconstruction.
2. **Loss Function Mismatch:** You are using an L1 loss (which encourages the conditional mean) alongside a spectral loss. This combination is mathematically contradictory for high-frequency recovery: the L1 loss forces the model to output the "blurry" mean of all possible high-frequency realizations to minimize error, while the spectral loss tries to force high-frequency power. This is likely why your transfer function is low—the model is "hedging" its bets.
3. **Missing Physical Metric:** You mention $Y_{SZ}-M$ scaling but have not yet reported the actual scatter ($\sigma_{\ln Y|M}$) or the slope bias. This is the most important metric for a paper. If the SR-DAE produces a cleaner map, the scatter in the $Y_{SZ}-M$ relation should be significantly lower than cILC, even if the pixel-wise phase is lost.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Pivot to Probabilistic Metrics:** Stop focusing on pixel-wise phase alignment ($T(\ell)$) at $\ell > 3000$. Instead, evaluate the **PDF of the reconstructed $Y_{SZ}$ values** for halos of different mass bins. If the SR-DAE recovers the correct distribution of pressure, it is a success, regardless of whether the specific pixel phases match the ground truth.
2. **Refine the Loss Function:** If you proceed with the SR-DAE, consider a **Perceptual Loss** (e.g., using a pre-trained VGG or a simple discriminator) instead of a pure spectral loss. This will encourage the model to generate "sharp" features that look like the simulation without requiring pixel-perfect phase matching.
3. **Prioritize the CDM:** Your results already show that the SR-DAE is "smoothing" the output to avoid L1 penalties. This is the classic behavior of a model that needs to be a generative sampler. **Execute the CDM immediately.** The CDM will allow you to sample the high-frequency modes that the SR-DAE currently suppresses. The paper's impact will be significantly higher if you demonstrate that the CDM recovers the correct *statistical* properties (power spectrum and $Y-M$ scatter) even when the deterministic SR-DAE fails to match the ground truth pixel-by-pixel.
4. **CIB Leakage Validation:** Your cross-correlation with CIB is a good start, but it is a linear metric. Since you suspect non-linear leakage, perform a **conditional scatter plot** of $R$ vs. CIB intensity. If the leakage is non-linear, the Pearson coefficient will underestimate the problem.

**Summary for Next Iteration:**
Do not spend more time trying to "fix" the transfer function. It is a physical limit. Shift the narrative: the SR-DAE is a "mean-field" estimator, and the CDM is the "full-distribution" estimator. The paper should focus on the *statistical* recovery of the $Y_{SZ}-M$ relation, which is the actual scientific goal.
