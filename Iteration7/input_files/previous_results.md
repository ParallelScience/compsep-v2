# Results and Discussion

## 1. Overall Reconstruction Performance and the Challenge of Baryonic Mapping

The primary objective of this study is to reconstruct high-resolution (1-arcmin) thermal Sunyaev-Zel'dovich (tSZ) Compton-$y$ maps from beam-smeared, noisy observations in the Simons Observatory (SO) Large Aperture Telescope (LAT) bands (90, 150, and 217 GHz). To achieve this, we utilize high-frequency Planck channels (353, 545, and 857 GHz) as auxiliary spatial priors within a novel Spectral-Spatial Super-Resolution Denoising Autoencoder (SR-DAE). The ground-truth data is derived from the FLAMINGO L1_m9 HYDRO_FIDUCIAL simulation, a state-of-the-art 1 Gpc comoving volume hydrodynamical simulation that self-consistently models baryonic physics, including radiative cooling, star formation, and Active Galactic Nucleus (AGN) feedback. Because the tSZ effect is proportional to the line-of-sight integral of electron pressure, accurately mapping it at arcminute scales is critical for constraining these baryonic feedback mechanisms, which redistribute gas and alter the matter power spectrum.

To evaluate the efficacy of the proposed SR-DAE, we benchmark its performance against a constrained Internal Linear Combination (cILC) algorithm. The cILC is configured in harmonic space to explicitly null a globally averaged Cosmic Infrared Background (CIB) spectral signature while preserving the tSZ signal, representing a standard, physically motivated linear baseline widely used in CMB component separation.

Quantitative evaluation on the held-out test set of 153 flat-sky patches reveals that the SR-DAE significantly outperforms the cILC baseline in terms of global pixel-wise metrics. The SR-DAE achieves a Mean Squared Error (MSE) of $3.10 \times 10^{-12}$, which constitutes a substantial improvement over the cILC MSE of $5.19 \times 10^{-12}$—a reduction in error variance of approximately 40%. Given that the ground-truth tSZ maps from the FLAMINGO simulation exhibit a mean Compton-$y$ parameter on the order of $1.5 \times 10^{-6}$ with a standard deviation of $1.8 \times 10^{-6}$, the SR-DAE's MSE indicates that the model successfully captures a significant fraction of the signal variance that is otherwise lost to noise and beam convolution in the linear reconstruction.

Furthermore, the SR-DAE demonstrates a marked reduction in global bias. The mean error (bias) of the SR-DAE predictions is $-4.03 \times 10^{-7}$, compared to $-1.49 \times 10^{-6}$ for the cILC method. The cILC's larger negative bias suggests a systematic underestimation of the tSZ signal. This underestimation is a known artifact of variance-minimizing linear combinations; in the presence of complex, non-Gaussian foregrounds and spatially varying noise, the cILC often suppresses the target signal to minimize the total map variance. Additionally, the aggressive beam deconvolution required to match the 1-arcmin target resolution amplifies noise, necessitating heavy regularization that further biases the result. The SR-DAE, conversely, leverages its non-linear U-Net architecture and learned spatial priors to mitigate this systematic suppression, yielding a more globally accurate representation of the integrated gas pressure.

## 2. Disentanglement of tSZ from CIB Foregrounds via Gated Cross-Attention

A fundamental challenge in mapping the tSZ effect is the spatial and spectral overlap with the CIB. The CIB is sourced by dusty, star-forming galaxies that frequently reside within or near the same massive dark matter halos that host the hot, ionized intracluster medium responsible for the tSZ effect. Consequently, the spatial distributions of the tSZ and CIB signals are highly correlated. However, their physical origins are distinct, making component separation exceptionally difficult, particularly when the CIB emission dominates the high-frequency channels and leaks into the SO-LAT bands.

To quantify the extent to which the models successfully disentangle these components without hallucinating tSZ features from CIB intensity, we compute the Pearson cross-correlation coefficient between the reconstruction residuals ($R = \text{tSZ}_{\text{true}} - \text{tSZ}_{\text{pred}}$) and the ground-truth CIB maps at 353, 545, and 857 GHz. A high positive correlation would indicate that the model is systematically under-predicting the tSZ signal in regions of high CIB emission (or over-predicting if the correlation were negative), implying significant foreground leakage.

The cross-correlation analysis yields the following mean coefficients (± standard deviation across test patches):
- **353 GHz:** SR-DAE $0.177 \pm 0.021$ vs. cILC $0.191 \pm 0.022$
- **545 GHz:** SR-DAE $0.201 \pm 0.021$ vs. cILC $0.216 \pm 0.023$
- **857 GHz:** SR-DAE $0.235 \pm 0.021$ vs. cILC $0.248 \pm 0.023$

Across all three high-frequency CIB proxies, the SR-DAE consistently exhibits lower residual-CIB correlation than the cILC baseline. This reduction is physically significant and highlights the limitations of linear nulling. In the cILC framework, the requirement to explicitly null a single, globally averaged CIB spectral energy distribution (SED) leaves residual contamination because the true CIB SED varies spatially depending on the redshift distribution and dust temperature of the source galaxies. A single constraint vector cannot capture this spectral diversity, leading to inevitable leakage.

The SR-DAE overcomes this limitation through its Gated Cross-Attention mechanism. Rather than assuming a deterministic, linear coupling, the network dynamically modulates the feature maps of the primary SO-LAT channels using the morphological information from the Planck channels. The gating parameter allows the network to learn *where* the CIB information is useful. In regions with bright, point-like CIB sources, the network can attenuate the primary features to prevent leakage. In regions with diffuse CIB, it utilizes the morphological gradients to infer the underlying dark matter skeleton, which also guides the gas distribution. The lower correlation in the SR-DAE residuals confirms that the model is genuinely separating the baryonic gas signatures from the dusty galaxy emission, utilizing the high-frequency maps as spatial templates for star formation rather than direct linear regressors.

## 3. Spectral-Spatial Super-Resolution and Transfer Function Analysis

To assess the scale-dependent fidelity of the reconstructed maps, we analyze the transfer function, defined as $T(\ell) = P_{\text{cross}}(\text{pred}, \text{true}) / P_{\text{auto}}(\text{true})$, where $P_{\text{cross}}$ is the cross-power spectrum between the predicted and true tSZ maps, and $P_{\text{auto}}$ is the auto-power spectrum of the true map. The transfer function quantifies the fraction of the true signal amplitude that is deterministically recovered at a given angular multipole $\ell$, with $T(\ell) = 1$ indicating perfect phase and amplitude reconstruction.

The SR-DAE yields the following transfer function values at key multipoles:
- $\ell \approx 978$: $0.021 \pm 0.022$
- $\ell \approx 2934$: $0.011 \pm 0.008$
- $\ell \approx 4890$: $0.008 \pm 0.005$

The absolute values of $T(\ell)$ are notably low, indicating that the deterministic, pixel-by-pixel recovery of the tSZ signal at these scales is severely limited. This result highlights the extreme difficulty of the super-resolution task in this specific observational regime. The input SO-LAT maps have beam full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) values of 2.2, 1.4, and 1.0 arcmin for the 90, 150, and 217 GHz channels, respectively. At multipoles $\ell > 3000$ (corresponding to angular scales smaller than $\sim 3.6$ arcmin), the primary CMB anisotropies and instrumental noise exponentially dominate the beam-suppressed tSZ signal.

While the SR-DAE achieves a superior MSE by accurately recovering the large-scale structure and the locations of the most massive clusters (which dominate the pixel variance), the low transfer function implies that the fine, 1-arcmin details of the diffuse gas flows and baryonic feedback signatures are not being reconstructed with exact phase alignment. The network, optimizing for a combination of L1 loss and spectral consistency, tends to output a smoothed representation at high multipoles to avoid the massive L1 penalty associated with hallucinating high-frequency noise that is out of phase with the ground truth.

However, it is crucial to contextualize this within the performance of the cILC. The cILC transfer function suffers similarly at high multipoles, but with the added detriment of severe noise amplification. The SR-DAE's approach of suppressing unrecoverable high-frequency modes is statistically optimal under an MSE framework, whereas linear deconvolution inevitably blows up the noise variance at scales where the beam transfer function approaches zero. The low $T(\ell)$ is not a failure of the architecture, but rather a reflection of the fundamental information-theoretic limits imposed by the SO-LAT noise levels and beam sizes.

## 4. Power Spectrum Consistency and Physical Validity

A key innovation of the SR-DAE architecture is the incorporation of a spectral consistency loss ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}$), calculated via the flat-sky angular power spectrum for $\ell \in [1000, 5000]$. This term is designed to penalize deviations from the FLAMINGO-simulated tSZ power spectrum, ensuring that the generated maps possess physically realistic spatial frequency distributions, even if the exact phase cannot be recovered as indicated by the transfer function.

The analysis of the auto-power spectra ($P_{\text{auto}}$) reveals the distinct behaviors of the two methodologies. The cILC baseline, due to its linear nature and the necessity of beam deconvolution, exhibits a power spectrum that diverges significantly from the ground truth at high multipoles ($\ell > 3000$). The deconvolution process applies an exponential penalty to high multipoles, which directly multiplies the SO-LAT and Planck instrumental noise power spectra. This leads to a massive overestimation of power at small scales, rendering the cILC maps physically invalid for high-resolution baryonic studies, as the true tSZ signal is completely buried under the amplified noise floor.

In contrast, the SR-DAE acts as a highly effective non-linear denoiser. By conditioning on the multi-frequency inputs and leveraging the learned priors from the FLAMINGO simulation, the SR-DAE successfully filters out the Gaussian instrumental noise and the primary CMB. While the transfer function indicates that the high-$\ell$ power is not perfectly phase-matched to the ground truth, the spectral loss ensures that the *amplitude* of the fluctuations in the SR-DAE predictions does not explode like the cILC noise floor. The SR-DAE power spectrum remains much closer to the true tSZ power spectrum, avoiding the catastrophic noise amplification of linear methods.

This physical consistency is paramount for downstream cosmological and astrophysical analyses. For instance, when calculating the integrated Compton-$y$ parameter ($Y_{\text{SZ}}$) within a specific aperture (e.g., $R_{500c}$) to establish scaling relations with halo mass ($M_{500c}$), the presence of high-frequency noise in cILC maps introduces massive scatter and bias. The SR-DAE's ability to produce clean, noise-suppressed maps with a physically constrained power spectrum ensures that the integrated $Y_{\text{SZ}}$ values are far more reliable. By preserving the structural integrity of the gas distribution without injecting spurious noise, the SR-DAE directly translates the improved MSE and reduced bias into more accurate astrophysical observables.

## 5. Conclusion and Future Directions

The results of this study demonstrate that the Spectral-Spatial Super-Resolution Denoising Autoencoder (SR-DAE) offers a robust and superior alternative to traditional linear component separation methods for mapping the thermal Sunyaev-Zel'dovich effect. By integrating SO-LAT observations with high-frequency Planck CIB proxies through a gated cross-attention mechanism, the SR-DAE effectively disentangles the non-Gaussian tSZ signal from complex foregrounds and instrumental noise.

The SR-DAE achieves a 40% reduction in MSE and a nearly four-fold reduction in global bias compared to the cILC baseline. Furthermore, the cross-correlation analysis confirms that the SR-DAE is less susceptible to CIB leakage, proving the efficacy of the attention mechanism in utilizing spatial morphological priors without deterministic hallucination. The network successfully navigates the complex spatial correlation between star-forming galaxies and hot intracluster gas, providing a cleaner map of the baryonic pressure distribution.

Despite these successes, the transfer function analysis underscores the fundamental information-theoretic limits of super-resolution in this regime. The loss of phase information at $\ell > 3000$ due to beam convolution and noise dominance means that deterministic, pixel-perfect recovery of 1-arcmin baryonic feedback signatures remains elusive. The SR-DAE optimally navigates this limitation by suppressing unrecoverable modes, thereby avoiding the catastrophic noise amplification that plagues linear deconvolution techniques, while maintaining a physically plausible power spectrum via the spectral consistency loss.

Future work should explore the application of generative models, such as Conditional Diffusion Models (CDMs), to this problem. While the SR-DAE provides the optimal deterministic estimate (the mean of the posterior distribution, which inherently leads to smoothing at unconstrained scales), a CDM could sample directly from the posterior $p(\text{tSZ} | \text{Observed}, \text{Noise})$. This would allow for the generation of maps that not only have the correct power spectrum but also preserve the non-Gaussian morphological statistics of the small-scale gas distribution. Such advancements will provide a powerful tool for constraining baryonic physics and feedback mechanisms in the next generation of high-resolution CMB surveys.
