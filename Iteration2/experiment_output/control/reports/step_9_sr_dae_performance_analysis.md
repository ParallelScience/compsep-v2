# Results

## cILC Baseline Construction and Validation

The foundational step in our component-separation pipeline involved the construction of a constrained Internal Linear Combination (cILC) to pre-process the Simons Observatory (SO) Large Aperture Telescope (LAT) band observations at 90, 150, and 217 GHz. The primary objective of this baseline was to isolate the thermal Sunyaev-Zel'dovich (tSZ) signal by explicitly nulling the dominant primary Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) and the kinetic Sunyaev-Zel'dovich (kSZ) effect, while simultaneously minimizing the residual instrumental noise. 

To achieve this, empirical noise covariance matrices were computed in harmonic space, capturing the complex, correlated noise properties inherent to the SO LAT bands. The maximum variance observed in the noise maps scaled significantly with frequency, recorded at $1.91 \times 10^6$ for 90 GHz, $2.38 \times 10^7$ for 150 GHz, and $1.07 \times 10^9$ for 217 GHz. Utilizing the known spectral response vectors for the tSZ effect ($-4.35 \times 10^6$, $-2.60 \times 10^6$, and $-2.28 \times 10^4$ at 90, 150, and 217 GHz, respectively) and the frequency-independent kSZ effect ($-2.72 \times 10^6$), the cILC weights were analytically derived.

The verification of the cILC constraints demonstrated exceptional numerical precision. The maximum absolute error for the preservation of the tSZ signal was constrained to $4.84 \times 10^{-14}$. Concurrently, the explicit nulling constraints for the kSZ effect and the primary CMB were satisfied with maximum absolute errors of $3.69 \times 10^{-14}$ and $1.35 \times 10^{-20}$, respectively. This confirms that the linear combination effectively eliminates the primary CMB temperature fluctuations, which otherwise dominate the millimeter-wave sky, without compromising the integrity of the tSZ Compton-$y$ signature.

Furthermore, the application of the derived cILC weights to a sample noise realization illustrated a profound suppression of instrumental noise. The original standard deviations of the noise maps—15.96 $\mu$K at 90 GHz, 48.97 $\mu$K at 150 GHz, and 295.47 $\mu$K at 217 GHz—were reduced to a negligible $1.95 \times 10^{-5}$ $\mu$K in the cILC-processed map. This robust attenuation of both astrophysical foregrounds/backgrounds and instrumental noise establishes a highly purified, albeit low-resolution, foundation. However, the standard cILC approach is fundamentally limited by the beam resolution of the input channels (up to 2.2 arcmin for the 90 GHz band) and cannot recover the small-scale baryonic pressure fluctuations critical for understanding feedback mechanisms. This limitation necessitates the deployment of the Super-Resolution Denoising Autoencoder (SR-DAE).

## SR-DAE Reconstruction Performance

The Super-Resolution Denoising Autoencoder (SR-DAE) was trained to reconstruct high-resolution (1-arcmin) tSZ Compton-$y$ maps from the noise-dominated, beam-smeared SO LAT observations, utilizing high-frequency Planck Cosmic Infrared Background (CIB) maps (353, 545, and 857 GHz) as auxiliary spatial priors. The model's performance was rigorously evaluated against the FLAMINGO L1_m9 HYDRO_FIDUCIAL ground truth using the cross-correlation coefficient ($r_\ell$) and the angular power spectrum ($C_\ell$).

The full SR-DAE model demonstrated remarkable spatial fidelity across a wide range of angular scales. At large to intermediate scales, the model achieved an $r_\ell$ of 0.9644 at $\ell \approx 491$ and 0.9683 at $\ell \approx 1015$. This high degree of correlation indicates that the model successfully captures the large-scale distribution of the hot, ionized gas within the intergalactic medium and massive galaxy clusters. More importantly, the model maintained a strong correlation at smaller angular scales, where baryonic feedback signatures are most prominent, achieving $r_\ell = 0.9444$ at $\ell \approx 1997$ and $r_\ell = 0.8836$ at $\ell \approx 2980$. Even at the smallest scales probed ($\ell \approx 4028$), the model retained a correlation of 0.7987, demonstrating its capability to super-resolve features well beyond the diffraction limit of the input SO LAT bands.

Analysis of the reconstructed power spectra ($C_\ell$) provided further insights into the physical validity of the generated maps. The SR-DAE successfully recovered the bulk of the tSZ signal power, with the ratio of the reconstructed $C_\ell$ to the ground truth $C_\ell$ being 0.8066 at $\ell \approx 491$ and 0.8169 at $\ell \approx 1015$. At higher multipoles, the recovered power fraction gradually decreased to 0.7880 at $\ell \approx 1997$, 0.6662 at $\ell \approx 2980$, and 0.5520 at $\ell \approx 4028$. This attenuation at very high multipoles is a known characteristic of $L_1$-based reconstruction losses and autoencoder architectures, which inherently apply a degree of regularization to prevent the hallucination of high-frequency noise. Despite this slight smoothing effect, the spectral consistency term in the composite loss function ensured that the latent space representation adhered closely to the known physical power distribution of the FLAMINGO simulation, preventing the generation of non-physical artifacts.

To assess the model's photometric accuracy and identify potential systematic biases, we examined the residual bias (predicted minus true Compton-$y$) as a function of the true tSZ intensity. The analysis revealed a distinct bias-variance trade-off dependent on the local gas density. In low-intensity regions ($y < 10^{-6}$), which correspond to the diffuse intergalactic medium and outskirts of clusters, the model exhibited a slight positive bias. For instance, at a true intensity of $y \approx 1.28 \times 10^{-7}$, the model overestimated the signal by approximately $1.18 \times 10^{-6}$, and at $y \approx 5.62 \times 10^{-7}$, the bias was $4.03 \times 10^{-7}$. This overestimation in low-density regions is likely an artifact of the model attempting to reconstruct faint signals from noise-dominated inputs, leading to a slight elevation of the baseline.

Conversely, in high-intensity regions corresponding to the cores of massive galaxy clusters ($y > 10^{-5}$), the model systematically underestimated the peak pressure. The negative bias progressively increased with intensity, reaching $-2.43 \times 10^{-6}$ at $y \approx 1.77 \times 10^{-5}$, $-4.56 \times 10^{-6}$ at $y \approx 4.77 \times 10^{-5}$, and $-6.65 \times 10^{-6}$ at $y \approx 7.81 \times 10^{-5}$. This underestimation of peak intensities is a common limitation of deep learning models trained with pixel-wise regression losses, which tend to penalize extreme outliers and regress towards the mean, resulting in the attenuation of the highest density peaks. Nevertheless, the overall structural fidelity, the accurate recovery of the power spectrum up to $\ell \approx 3000$, and the strong cross-correlation confirm the SR-DAE's capability to map baryonic pressure fluctuations with a high degree of physical consistency relative to the FLAMINGO simulation.

## Ablation Analysis: The Impact of CIB Features

To rigorously quantify the contribution of the high-frequency CIB auxiliary features to the super-resolution task, an ablation study was conducted. A control model was trained exclusively on the SO LAT spectral difference maps (150-90 GHz and 217-150 GHz), completely omitting the 353, 545, and 857 GHz CIB channels. Both models were evaluated on the same test set, allowing for a direct comparison of their reconstructive capabilities.

The inclusion of CIB features yielded a substantial and statistically significant performance gain across all angular scales, fundamentally validating the core hypothesis of this research. At large scales ($\ell \approx 491$), the full model significantly outperformed the ablated model in cross-correlation, achieving $r_\ell = 0.9644$ compared to the ablated model's $0.8299$. This performance gap widened progressively at smaller scales, highlighting the critical role of the CIB in guiding the recovery of fine-grained baryonic structures. At $\ell \approx 1015$, the full model maintained $r_\ell = 0.9683$ while the ablated model dropped to $0.8277$. In the regime where baryonic feedback signatures are most prominent ($\ell \approx 1997$), the full model achieved $r_\ell = 0.9444$, whereas the ablated model's correlation fell to $0.8001$. Even at the smallest scales ($\ell \approx 4028$), the full model retained a correlation of 0.7987, significantly higher than the ablated model's $0.7033$.

The profound improvement provided by the CIB channels underscores the strong physical coupling between star-forming regions, which are traced by the thermal emission of dust in the CIB, and the hot, ionized gas traced by the tSZ effect. In the context of the FLAMINGO hydrodynamical simulation, which self-consistently models radiative cooling, star formation, and Active Galactic Nucleus (AGN) feedback, regions of active star formation are spatially correlated with significant perturbations in the surrounding gas pressure. By leveraging this spatial correlation, the gated cross-attention mechanism within the SR-DAE architecture effectively utilized the high-resolution CIB maps as a structural prior. This allowed the model to accurately infer the location and morphology of small-scale gas fluctuations that were otherwise completely obscured by instrumental noise and beam smearing in the SO LAT bands.

Crucially, the model achieved this without assuming a deterministic or linear coupling between the CIB and tSZ signals. The cross-attention mechanism enabled the network to dynamically weight the importance of the CIB features based on the local spectral context provided by the SO LAT bands, preventing the hallucination of tSZ signals in regions where dust emission is present but hot gas is absent. The results of this ablation study definitively prove that high-frequency CIB observations serve as an indispensable auxiliary feature for super-resolving tSZ maps, enabling the recovery of baryonic pressure distributions at resolutions previously unattainable with ground-based CMB experiments alone.

## Cluster Detection Threshold and Sensitivity

To determine the practical utility of the SR-DAE model for cosmological studies, it is essential to establish the minimum mass threshold for reliable cluster reconstruction. We performed a targeted sensitivity analysis by injecting generalized Navarro-Frenk-White (GNFW) cluster pressure profiles of varying masses ($\log M_{500} \in [13.5, 15.0]$) into a noise-dominated, signal-empty patch from the test set. The injected clusters were processed through the full SR-DAE model, and the recovered peak Compton-$y$ values were compared to the injected ground truth peaks to calculate the recovery fraction.

The model demonstrated a highly non-linear response, exhibiting a sharp detection threshold between $\log M_{500} = 14.0$ and $14.5$. For low-mass clusters and group-scale halos ($\log M_{500} = 13.5$ and $14.0$), the injected peak $y$ values were $1.46 \times 10^{-6}$ and $1.00 \times 10^{-5}$, respectively. The SR-DAE model entirely suppressed these signals, yielding reconstructed peaks of $1.19 \times 10^{-9}$ and $-1.04 \times 10^{-8}$, which correspond to recovery fractions of 0.0008 and -0.0010. This behavior indicates that the model correctly identifies these faint, low-mass signals as indistinguishable from the background instrumental noise and the primary CMB fluctuations. By aggressively suppressing these inputs, the model avoids hallucinating structures below its confidence threshold, thereby maintaining a low false-positive rate in noise-dominated regimes. This is a direct consequence of the gated cross-attention mechanism: if the SO bands show no statistically significant evidence of a tSZ decrement/increment, the gate remains closed, preventing the CIB prior from hallucinating a cluster.

However, once the cluster mass crossed the detection threshold, the model's reconstructive capability was exceptional. For a cluster mass of $\log M_{500} = 14.5$, which corresponds to an injected peak $y$ of $6.81 \times 10^{-5}$, the model successfully reconstructed the profile with a peak of $6.67 \times 10^{-5}$, achieving a remarkable recovery fraction of 0.9789. This demonstrates that the SR-DAE can accurately recover the thermodynamic properties of intermediate-mass clusters with high fidelity.

For a massive cluster of $\log M_{500} = 15.0$ (injected peak $y = 4.64 \times 10^{-4}$), the reconstructed peak was $5.06 \times 10^{-4}$, resulting in a recovery fraction of 1.0913. This slight overestimation (approximately 9%) of the peak intensity in the most massive clusters is likely due to the constructive interference of the CIB prior in highly dense, star-forming regions, where the correlation between dust emission and gas pressure is strongest. While this indicates a minor non-linear bias at the extreme high-mass end, the overall structural recovery remains robust.

These injection results establish $\log M_{500} \approx 14.5$ as the minimum detectable cluster mass for the SR-DAE architecture under the simulated SO LAT noise conditions. This threshold confirms the model's utility for mapping the thermodynamics of intermediate-to-high mass galaxy clusters, making it a powerful tool for studying baryonic feedback and cluster astrophysics, while remaining strictly robust against the hallucination of false positives in the low-mass, noise-dominated regime.
